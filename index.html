Airline Safety: Two Eras of Data

How has commercial aviation safety changed over time? Using the FiveThirtyEight airline safety dataset we compare incident counts and fatalities across two 15-year windows — before and after the year 2000.

Graphic 1 — Safety Improvement: Incidents by Era (Top 10 Largest Airlines)

This grouped bar chart shows the total number of reported incidents for the ten largest airlines (ranked by available seat-kilometres per week) across two 15-year periods: 1985 – 1999 and 2000 – 2014.

Why it's important: The dramatic drop in incidents at nearly every carrier is one of the clearest illustrations of how aviation safety has improved over the past four decades. Advances in aircraft technology, stricter international regulations, improved crew training (CRM), and better maintenance practices all contributed to this trend. Seeing the comparison side-by-side for the world's busiest airlines makes the progress concrete and easy to grasp.

Graphic 2 — Flight Volume vs. Fatalities (All Airlines)

Each point in this scatter plot represents a single airline. The horizontal axis shows how large that airline is (available seat-km per week — a proxy for total flying activity), while the vertical axis shows its total fatalities. Trend lines are drawn for each era: 1985 – 1999 and 2000 – 2014.

Why it's interesting: You might expect bigger airlines to have far more fatalities simply because they fly more. The chart shows this relationship is surprisingly weak — several very large carriers recorded zero fatalities in the modern era. The downward shift of the 2000–2014 trend line relative to 1985–1999 reinforces that flying became safer even as the volume of air travel grew enormously.
